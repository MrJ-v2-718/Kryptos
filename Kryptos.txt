In textual studies, a palimpsest (/ˈpælɪmpsɛst/) is a 
manuscript page, either from a scroll or a book, from

preparation for reuse[1] in the form of another document.
[2] Parchment was made of lamb, calf, or kid skin and was 
expensive and not readily available, so, in the interest 
of economy, a page was often re-used by scraping off the 
previous writing. In colloquial usage, the term palimpsest 
is also used in architecture, archaeology and geomorphology 
to denote an object made or worked upon for one purpose and 
later reused for another; for example, a monumental brass the 
reverse blank side of which has been re-engraved.

The Clock of Flowing Time
-------------------------
(German: Uhr der fließenden Zeit)[1] is a 13 metres (43 ft) high water clock extending over three floors in the Berlin Europa-Center. The clock was designed by the French artist Bernard Gitton and set up in 1982.

The water clock displays the time by filling glass spheres with brightly colored liquid, in a cycle that repeats every 12 hours. The whole system is controlled by a pendulum swinging in the lower half of the clock.

text from Kryptos with slight differences.
